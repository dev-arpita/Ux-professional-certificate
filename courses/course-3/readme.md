Wireframes are essential, low-fidelity blueprints in UX design that outline the structure, layout, and functionality of a digital product, such as websites or apps, without using colors or detailed graphics.

### Use Gestalt Principles when creating wireframes
 Gestalt principles describe how humans group similar elements, recognize patterns, and simplify complex images when
we perceive objects. You can use the principles to organize content on apps and websites so it's visually pleasing and easier
to understand.

### low-fidelity prototypes
Low-fidelity prototypes are simple interactive models that give a basic idea of the final product's look and feel. The key difference is interactivity; low-fidelity prototypes allow users to click through screens, enhancing user testing.

### Deceptive patterns in UX design
**Deceptive patterns **(originally called "dark patterns") are user interface designs specifically crafted to trick or manipulate users into taking actions they might not otherwise choose, such as making unintended purchases, sharing personal data, or subscribing to services.
(read more..)[https://docs.google.com/document/d/1JpeZ2e6P3q4sutu4Mt5phHTzFetngU4swT-HAdfUJXI/edit?tab=t.em2eyen7ubd]
(Avoid deceptive patterns)[https://docs.google.com/document/d/1JpeZ2e6P3q4sutu4Mt5phHTzFetngU4swT-HAdfUJXI/edit?tab=t.5sqbgaiaf5q4]

### The effects of designs that deceive
Deceptive designs prioritize immediate metrics over user well-being, resulting in a toxic user experience that erodes brand equity. These patterns trigger emotional distress like guilt or frustration, increase cognitive load by forcing users to navigate traps, and lead to financial loss through hidden costs or difficult cancellations. Beyond damaging customer loyalty and causing high churn rates, these tactics now carry massive legal risks, as global regulators increasingly impose heavy fines for manipulative interfaces.
